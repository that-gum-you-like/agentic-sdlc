# Jony Ive Product Design

> "Design is not just what it looks like and feels like. Design is how it works." — Steve Jobs
>
> "You have to deeply understand the essence of a product in order to be able to get rid of the parts that are not essential." — Jony Ive

This skill is a way of *thinking*, not a checklist to rush through. It is the design methodology that produced the iMac, iPod, iPhone, and the unibody Mac: an obsessive pursuit of the *essence* of a thing, the discipline to remove everything that isn't essential, and the care to refine what remains until the result feels **inevitable** — as if no other solution were ever possible.

It is **distinct from UI/visual design.** Visual design asks "how should this look." This skill asks "what should this *be*, what is it *for*, and how should it *work*." Get this right and the surface design becomes almost obvious. Get it wrong and no amount of polish will save it.

Use this whenever you are: designing a new product, feature, or experience from scratch · improving or simplifying an existing one · shaping a future roadmap · running a design critique · or making any product-design decision from first principles.

---

## The Core Philosophy

Hold these five beliefs. Everything below flows from them.

1. **Design is how it works, not how it looks.** The look is a *consequence* of resolving how it works. If you start with the surface, you've already lost. Start with purpose and behavior.

2. **Simplicity is not the absence of clutter — it is the presence of clarity.** Removing things from a confused product just gives you a cleaner confused product. True simplicity comes from understanding a thing so completely that its form becomes obvious. *"Simplicity is somehow essentially describing the purpose and place of an object."*

3. **Care is the moral core.** What you make testifies to who you are. People can feel care, and they can feel carelessness — including in the parts they'll never consciously see. Carelessness is not neutral; it is a small disrespect to the person using the thing.

4. **Get to inevitable.** Keep going — past the first workable answer, past clever, past novel — until you reach the point where you say *"of course,"* where there is no rational alternative. That feeling of inevitability is the target, and it is *hard*.

5. **Defer to the user.** Good design quietly serves the task. Bad design performs for its maker — "designers wagging their tails in your face." When someone struggles with a product, they blame themselves; that self-blame is the design's fault, not theirs. Earn their trust by getting out of the way.

These are demanding. Most products fail them not because the team lacked talent but because they stopped too early. **The work is in not stopping early.**

## The Design Process — Five Phases

This is a loop, not an assembly line. You will revisit earlier phases as later ones teach you something. But do not *skip* phases — especially not Phase 1. Skipping the essence is the single most common cause of bad products.

### Phase 1 — Understand the Essence
*Goal: be able to state, in one sentence, what this thing fundamentally is and why it deserves to exist.*

Before any feature list, any sketch, any spec — understand the human truth underneath. Not "what features should it have" but "what is the one thing this exists to do, and what does the person actually want when they reach for it?"

Ask:
- If this product is the answer, **what is the question?** State it as a human need, not a feature.
- What is the *one job* it does? If you can't name it in a sentence, you don't understand it yet.
- What is the person *really* trying to accomplish — the need behind the stated request?
- What would the world feel like if this didn't exist? What gap is real?
- Strip away everything trendy, everything competitors do, everything you assume — what's *left* that is essential?

**You cannot simplify what you don't understand.** Reduction (Phase 2) is impossible until the essence is clear. Spend disproportionate time here. The iPhone began with the essence "the whole front is the product, and it becomes whatever the software needs" — not with a feature list.

**Output of this phase:** a single, sharp sentence of essence. Write it down. Every later decision is judged against it.

### Phase 2 — Identify the Essential Functions
*Goal: ruthless reduction to the parts that earn their place.*

Now subtract. With the essence clear, almost everything is optional. Interrogate every function, every part, every feature:

- **Does this need to exist?** If we removed it, what actually breaks?
- **Can one thing do the job of four?** Collapse parts. (The unibody replaced dozens of components with one.) Collapse features. Collapse steps.
- **What are we saying NO to?** What you exclude is as important as what you include. A deliberate, defended "no" is a design act, not a failure.
- Is this here because the *user* needs it, or because it was easy to add, or because a competitor has it, or because someone's afraid to cut it? Only the first reason survives.
- If we could ship only **one** capability, which one? Build outward from that, reluctantly.

Beware: reduction is not amputation. You are not removing things to hit a number; you are removing everything that obscures the essence so what remains can be felt clearly. **Remove until it breaks, then add back the last thing.**

**Output:** the minimal set of functions, each one defended. A list of explicit, deliberate "no"s.

### Phase 3 — Material & Form
*Goal: find the form that the essence and its medium want to take.*

Form follows essence — *through* the honest nature of the material. In physical products the material is aluminum, glass, plastic. In software and experiences the "material" is interaction, time, attention, data, motion, the platform's native behaviors. **You cannot design honestly in a material you haven't worked with your hands** — so prototype in the real medium early, not in slides.

- What is the **honest** form for this? Let the medium be what it is. Don't make software pretend to be leather; don't make a digital thing fake a physical one without reason. Don't disguise structure as decoration.
- What is the *natural* shape of the interaction? The form should feel like it grew from the function, not like it was applied on top.
- Where does complexity *have* to live, and how do we order it rather than hide it? "Bringing order to complexity" — not pretending the complexity isn't there.
- What's the first physical/working manifestation we can make? **Make it.** Everything changes the moment the idea becomes real and you can hold it.

This is where you discover that the idea in your head doesn't survive contact with reality — which is the point. Welcome it.

**Output:** a working prototype (the lowest-fidelity thing that is *real*, not a mockup) embodying the form.

### Phase 4 — Obsessive Refinement
*Goal: resolve every detail, including the ones no one will ever see.*

This is where good becomes great, and where most teams quit too soon. Iterate relentlessly. Apple built *hundreds* of models per product. Assume your first prototype is 20% of the way there.

- **Care about the unseen.** The inside of the enclosure. The empty state. The error path. The thing that happens at 2am when the network drops. The user may never consciously notice — but they will *feel* whether it was cared for.
- Sweat the transitions and the in-between moments. Polish is not the surface color; it's how one state becomes another.
- Every detail must be deliberate. "Good enough" is a decision to be careless. If you can't say *why* it is the way it is, it isn't designed yet — it's defaulted.
- **Fail fast and often.** 80% of what you try won't work. That's not waste; that's how you find the 20%. If you're not failing, you're not pushing.
- Hold the bar even when it's expensive. Apple chose a Mac mini 2mm smaller than ideal, accepting a costlier drive, to keep the form right. Small early decisions define the whole product.

**Output:** a resolved design where you can defend every detail, and where removing anything more would break it.

### Phase 5 — Integration
*Goal: make it one coherent whole, part of a family, that feels inevitable.*

The final test is wholeness. Does it feel like one thing, made by one mind, with one point of view — or like a committee's negotiated settlement?

- **Coherence:** do all the parts feel like they belong together and to *this* product? Does hardware/software/experience resolve into a single intent?
- **Family resemblance:** does it feel like it belongs with the rest of what you make? (See `references/design-language.md` — the through-line that should persist across every product *regardless of the underlying CSS or implementation*.)
- **The whole experience:** the first run, the unboxing, the empty state, the moment of first delight, the offboarding. The product is everything around the product, too.
- **The inevitability test:** show it to yourself fresh. Does it feel contrived, or does it feel like *"of course, what else would it be?"* If contrived, you're not done — return to the phase where the contrivance entered.

**Output:** a shippable whole that feels calm, inevitable, and unmistakably part of the family.

## Design Principles

These are the standards every decision is measured against. When stuck, ask which principle the decision serves — and which it betrays.

- **Inevitable design.** The best designs feel inevitable, as if no other solution were possible. Push past the first answer until you reach "of course." Contrivance is a signal you stopped too early.

- **Quiet confidence.** Great design doesn't shout, doesn't decorate to impress, doesn't need to prove how clever it is. It is calm. It trusts the user to notice the care without being told. Restraint reads as confidence; ornament reads as insecurity.

- **Honesty.** Be true to the material and true to the function. No fake textures, no decoration masquerading as structure, no feature theater, no dark patterns, no pretending to be something you're not. Honest design respects both the medium and the person.

- **Respect for the user.** Defer to them. Never make them feel stupid. When they struggle, the design failed — fix the design, not the user. Care about their time, their attention, their trust, and the parts of the experience they'll never see.

- **Simplicity as clarity.** Not fewer things for the sake of fewer — clarity of purpose so complete that the form becomes obvious. Bring order to complexity; don't just hide it.

- **Care in the unseen.** Resolve the details no one will inspect. It's the difference between a product that was made and one that was *crafted*. People feel it even when they can't name it.

- **The courage to say no.** Focus is choosing what *not* to do. Every "yes" to a feature is a "no" to clarity. Defend your subtractions as fiercely as your additions.

- **Genuinely better, not superficially different.** Different and new is easy. Better is hard. Novelty for its own sake is vanity. Ask: is this actually better for the person, or just different?

## The Critique Process

Use this to evaluate any design — yours or someone else's, a new concept or a shipped product. Critique is not about taste or opinion; it is about holding work against the philosophy and finding where it falls short. Be rigorous and kind: hard on the work, respectful of the maker.

### How to run a critique
1. **Restate the essence.** What is this trying to be, in one sentence? If the team can't answer, stop here — that's the finding.
2. **Test against the essence.** Does every part serve it? Point at anything that doesn't.
3. **Walk the whole experience**, including the unseen paths — empty states, errors, first run, edge cases, the 2am failure.
4. **Apply the principles** one by one (inevitable? honest? deferential? simple-as-clarity? cared-for?).
5. **Hunt for the contrivance.** Where does it feel forced, bolted-on, negotiated? Trace each back to the decision that caused it.
6. **Separate the "is it good" from "is it done."** Name what's working, name what's unresolved, and be honest about which.

### Warning signs of poor design (red flags)
When you see these, name them plainly:

- 🚩 **No clear essence.** Nobody can say in one sentence what it's for. It's a pile of features, not a product.
- 🚩 **Feature creep / "and also."** Capability added because it was easy, or a competitor had it, or no one was brave enough to cut it.
- 🚩 **Decoration over purpose.** Visual flourish, animation, or ornament that serves the designer's ego, not the user's understanding.
- 🚩 **Designer wagging its tail.** You can *feel* the design performing for attention rather than serving the task.
- 🚩 **Dishonest material.** Fakery, skeuomorphism without reason, surfaces pretending to be something they aren't, dark patterns.
- 🚩 **The user blames themselves.** People struggle and assume *they're* the problem. That's a design failure wearing a disguise.
- 🚩 **Carelessness in the unseen.** Sloppy empty states, broken errors, unconsidered edges. If these are neglected, the visible parts were probably lucky, not designed.
- 🚩 **Superficially different.** New and flashy but not actually better for anyone.
- 🚩 **Negotiated incoherence.** Feels like a committee compromise — no single point of view, parts that don't belong together.
- 🚩 **Contrivance.** It feels forced. The opposite of inevitable. Something earlier in the process went wrong.
- 🚩 **Complexity hidden, not ordered.** The mess is still there, just swept under a clean-looking surface; it leaks the moment you go off the happy path.

A fuller evaluation rubric and a ready-to-use review template are in `references/critique-checklist.md`.

## Applying This Skill

### Designing a new product/feature from scratch
Run the five phases in order. Spend the most time in **Phase 1 (Essence)** — resist the urge to jump to solutions. Produce: a one-sentence essence, a defended minimal function set with explicit "no"s, a working prototype in the real medium, then refine obsessively, then integrate. Judge the result with the critique process before declaring it done.

### Improving / simplifying an existing product
Start at **Phase 1 anyway** — re-derive the essence, because the product has probably drifted from it. Then run the critique process to find where it betrays the principles. Most improvements are *subtractions*: what can be removed, collapsed, or clarified so the essence is felt again? Resist adding features to fix a product that's suffering from too many. Ask "what would this be if we designed it today, knowing what we know?"

### Developing a future roadmap
A roadmap is a sequence of *no*s as much as yes's. Anchor it to the enduring essence, not to chasing features. For each candidate: does it serve the essence, or dilute it? Sequence by what makes the product more *itself*, not more *crowded*. A great roadmap makes the product simpler and clearer over time, not just bigger. Protect coherence — every addition must still feel like family.

### Conducting a design critique
Use **The Critique Process** above with `references/critique-checklist.md`. Lead with the essence question. Be specific (point at the decision, not the person). Distinguish "unresolved" from "wrong." Always trace a flaw back to where it entered, so the fix addresses the cause.

### Thinking through a single design decision
Frame it against the essence and the principles. Ask: which option feels more *inevitable*? Which is more honest? Which defers more to the user? Which says the braver "no"? When two options seem equal, the simpler-in-clarity one usually wins. If neither feels right, the real problem is usually upstream — you haven't understood the essence well enough yet.

### Maintaining a consistent feel across all products
This is the "standard look" — a recognizable family resemblance that persists **even across completely different implementations, stacks, or CSS.** It is not a stylesheet; it is a set of experiential values (restraint, honesty, calm, deference, care, inevitability) made concrete. Every product you make should *feel* like it came from the same hand. The portable design language and its checklist live in `references/design-language.md` — apply it in Phase 5 (Integration) and whenever you start a new product.

## A Final Word on Posture

When you use this skill, slow down. The instinct is to generate options and move on. This methodology asks the opposite: understand more deeply, remove more bravely, refine more obsessively, and don't stop at workable. The goal is not a *good* product. The goal is a product that feels **inevitable** — and that only comes from caring more than is reasonable about things most people would never notice.
